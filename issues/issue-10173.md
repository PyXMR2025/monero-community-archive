# Original Description
We all know that when transferring XMR (or any other cryptocurrency) to a CEX, an anonymous transfer is of little significance, as the exchange will certainly know who you are and how much money you have received. Therefore, could an **optional** feature be added to allow users to use plaintext transactions when transferring funds to KYC-compliant custodial wallets, such as those used by CEXs (**Off by default**).

Meanwhile, since transfers between non-custodial XMR wallets would still maintain the default anonymity, this approach could potentially allow more CEX listings without compromising XMR's privacy protection.

From a usability perspective, I believe a better approach would be as follows: 

1. Add a flag to the wallet address to indicate that this wallet only accepts transactions where the `tx_extra` field contains proof of source (and is encrypted with the recipient's public key). 

2. When transferring funds from the paying wallet to such an address, provide a clear warning that the recipient of this transfer will know the paying wallet's address, and only proceed with the transfer after the user explicitly confirms they are aware of this.

This only adds one confirmation step for the user (and the confirmation is only needed when transferring to a CEX address), simplifying the process for both CEXs and users. Otherwise, expecting the user to correctly generate a proof of source using their private key and then encrypting the data with the recipient's public key would be unrealistic. With such complex operational steps, even if compliant, CEXs would have no interest in re-listing XMR.

## asbai | 2025-12-21T17:46:37+00:00
> Why to compromise that?

Monero users ultimately need to convert XMR back into USDT, USDC, or other fiat-pegged assets, right? If XMR becomes completely impossible to exchange for fiat, it will essentially lose almost all value. While you can certainly swap XMR for USDT through channels like DEXs, the USDT or USDC flowing from these sources will be tracked by all compliant financial institutions. They would likely reject the funds, or freeze them and trigger a rigorous AML review process.

Only through compliant integration with regulated financial institutions, such as CEXs, can you legally convert XMR into 'clean' and usable USDT/USDC or other fiat-linked assets. **Otherwise, the XMR you hold will be difficult to circulate through normal channels.**

> Plus, it will compromise Monero's overall privacy.

Not really. That’s just an optional feature that is disabled by default. Even if it were enabled, the CEX already knows your identity (via KYC) and exactly how much XMR you’ve received; letting them see which specific wallet address the funds came from doesn't actually reveal any additional privacy. **If you truly believe the sender's address is private, you can easily solve this by creating a new temporary one-time wallet to act as a 'final hop' before sending to the CEX.**

**So it’s obvious: technically, this new feature won't compromise Monero's privacy protections. At most, it might slightly affect its marketing appeal to 'privacy absolutists.' And it preserves the channels for Monero’s compliant circulation.**
