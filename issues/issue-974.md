"""
It seemed to me an attack on the Monero project. Binance delisting, while disabling withdrawals and at the same time telling everyone that perhaps it would not be able to pay the price for the remaining Monero, had all the characteristics of an attack. By telling it would not be able to pay the price in USD for the remaining Monero, it was creating insecurity on investors, who would only have the alternative to sell it. To me, seems like Binance was forced into pressuring the crypto community to abandon Monero. If this was an attack, however, it was very stupid, because instead of having only one major centralized entity negociating Monero, now that market share was taken by multiple smaller swappers/exchanges, who are profiting more from it. So by attacking and delisting Monero, the entity behind this attack ceded his dominance over the market and decentralized the community even further, making it stronger, really. If someone wanted to attack Monero, they should have made easier to transact it on Binance, where they could not only manipulate price, but also control information.

What would people think if Monero went back to 160 USD? after all this drama, having no effect on price? demand would show up, because it would have been proven the antifragility of Monero. Then, if people want to buy it, where are they gonna get it from?

So for us, our partners are still able to find and sell monero, and to buy it back as well. In the end, even if all major CEXs delist Monero, all minor exchanges would still be able to find liquidity with private people, such as hodlers and whales. In the end, people that hold large sums of Monero would still be able to provide liquidity to ChangeNow, SimpleSwap, etc. So governments would have to ban Monero itself, a.k.a., banning free speech, in order to make this illegal. And even if they do that, they only prove the point of the need for Monero.

"""
